High-Order Finite Element Method for Cardiac Ablation

Track Finite element methods

Presenting speaker Sumit Kumar (Institute of Information Science, The University of Kansas)

Authors Sumit Kumar (Institute of Information Science, The University of Kansas, Lawrence, Kansas, USA); Suzanne M. Shontz (Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, Department of Mechanical Engineering, Bioengineering Program, and the Institute for Information Sciences, The University of Kansas, Lawrence, Kansas, USA)

Abstract

Heart rhythm disorders (i.e., arrhythmias) are caused by abnormal electrical behavior of the heart, leading to poor contraction, blood pooling, and the formation of clots. Radio-frequency ablation is an effective treatment of arrhythmias that delivers thermal energy to the disorder sites, inducing thermal damage that electrically isolates the arrhythmia pathway. Successful treatment requires irreversible tissue damage by exposing the tissue to a temperature that causes cell death. Hence, the temperature distribution is critical for accurately predicting thermal effects. Traditional finite element methods for simulating thermal behavior during ablation therapy are computationally prohibitive for intra-operative thermal characterization. To develop an accurate and computationally efficient model for cardiac ablation, a higher-order finite element method is employed. A high-order mesh is first generated from the image voxels, yielding a hexahedral volumetric mesh and a quadrilateral surface mesh. Then, Pennes' bioheat equation is solved using the high-order finite element approach on the generated high-order mesh. The advantage of this approach is a computationally efficient solution scheme that relies on a small number of mesh elements to achieve the desired level of accuracy.
