David C. Del Rey Fernández

Associate Professor, Department of Applied Mathematics, University of Waterloo

The summation by parts framework: A matrix analysis approach to developing high-order methods with provable properties.

Abstract

Simulation tools, and the numerical methods that power them, are essential in modern science and engineering permeating the design and analysis of the simplest to the most sophisticated engineering systems. Simulation tools are used throughout product development cycles, from the ideation phase all the way through final product design and certification. Modern high-performance computing systems and future computing systems like quantum computers promise the power to drive unprecedented simulations of time-dependent nonlinear PDEs and therefore significantly push the frontiers of science and engineering. A natural choice to take advantage of this compute power are high-order methods, as they are more accurate and better able to leverage these systems than low-order methods. Despite this, their uptake in industry has been historically low as a result of robustness issues. Nevertheless, in the last 10-15 years significant progress has been made in the high-order community in developing schemes that retain the type of robustness required in industry.

In this talk, I will examine the contributions of the SBP community to developing high-order schemes that provide mathematical guarantees and therefore are a significant step towards industrialization of high-order methods. I will provide a high-level introduction to the SBP framework and current state-of-the-art developments, including shock-tracking schemes, exponentially-stable schemes, asymptotic preserving schemes, entropy-stable discretizations, quantum PDE methods, and machine-learning-accelerated simulation workflows.

Bio

David C. Del Rey Fernández is an Associate Professor in the Department of Applied Mathematics at the University of Waterloo. He is the Pratt & Whitney Canada Research Chair in Industrial Artificial Intelligence, Associate Director of the Waterloo Institute for Sustainable Aeronautics, and a Research Cluster Lead for the Future Cities Institute. He received the Industrial Mathematics Society Early Career Award (2024) and the Faculty of Mathematics Golden Jubilee Research Excellence Award (2025).

He obtained his PhD from the University of Toronto Institute for Aerospace Studies (2015) and was a postdoctoral fellow and research scientist at the National Institute of Aerospace and NASA Langley Research Center (2016–2021).

His research focuses on numerical methods for PDEs with mathematical guarantees, particularly the summation-by-parts framework, entropy-stable schemes, adaptive high-order methods, and emerging work on quantum PDE algorithms and machine-learning methods with provable properties.
