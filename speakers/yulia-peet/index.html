Yulia Peet

Associate Professor of Aerospace and Mechanical Engineering, Arizona State University

Discontinuous Galerkin Spectral Element Methods with Summation-by-Parts and Shock Capturing

Abstract

In this talk, we discuss the implementation of provably stable discretization schemes for shocked flows with spectral element methodologies. We first present the entropy-stable discontinuous Galerkin spectral element method (DGSEM) for compressible Euler and Navier-Stokes equations. We build our entropy-stable formulation on a split form of the convective terms and employ diagonal-norm summation-by-parts operators, which guarantee entropy-stability properties in high-Mach-number continuous flows. We then proceed to introducing two shock-capturing schemes that retain high-order accuracy in smooth regions while preserving entropy stability. The first scheme is based on an entropy viscosity formulation, while the second implements a hybrid sub-element order reduction by blending a high-order DG flux with a low-order sub-cell finite-volume flux. The performance of the two schemes is rigorously compared on a number of laminar and turbulent flow examples featuring weak and strong shocks at various Mach number regimes, and their relative advantages and disadvantages are highlighted. We finally demonstrate an ability of a split-form DGSEM method to resolve shock-turbulence interactions in a supersonic flow over a sphere.

Bio

Yulia Peet is an Associate Professor of Aerospace and Mechanical Engineering at the School for Engineering of Matter, Transport and Energy at Arizona State University, which she joined in Fall 2012. Her Ph.D. degree is in Aeronautics and Astronautics from Stanford (2006), M.S. and B.S. degrees are from Moscow Institute of Physics and Technology (1999 and 1997). Her previous appointments include a Postdoctoral position at the University of Pierre and Marie Curie in Paris in 2006-2008, and a dual appointment as an NSF Fellow at Northwestern and an Assistant Computational Scientist at Argonne National Laboratory in 2009-2012. Dr. Peet is a recipient of a National Science Foundation CAREER award in 2020 and an ASME Rising Star award in 2024. Her research interests include computational methods and high-performance computing, fluid mechanics and turbulence, with applications in aerospace, wind energy, and biological fluid mechanics.
